Targeted sensor placement: DQS for salinity fields

A scale-free quality score that puts greedy acquisition functions and a global differential-evolution optimiser on the same axis — and quantifies the cost of one-step-ahead selection.

Independent research UBC Apr 2026

Download full paper (PDF)↓

Summary

Salinity along an estuary is steep near the freshwater inflow and nearly flat offshore. Operators have a budget of three to twenty fixed probes and one practical question: where does the next sensor go?

Greedy acquisition functions are the standard tool, with a tidy $(1 - 1/e)$ guarantee — but that guarantee is on information-theoretic objectives, not on the downstream reconstruction error inside the scientifically critical sub-region. I built a framework that scores any sensor design against a problem-specific reference and used it to measure the structural gap between greedy and globally optimised placement.

// headline result
The noise-averaged DE optimiser attains DQS = 0.937 at N = 20; the strongest greedy heuristic (MaxDist) reaches only 0.675. The structural gap of 0.26 DQS — about 28% of achievable quality — peaks at 0.47 in the mid-budget regime (N = 10) and only narrows once MaxDist has finally filled the halocline.

Key technical ideas

The Dataset Quality Score

Let $\hat f_S$ be the GP posterior mean given sensors $S$, and let $\mathcal X_\text{steep} \subset \mathcal X$ be the steep sub-region. The gradient-weighted RMSE on the steep region is:

$$ E_\text{steep}(S) \;=\; \sqrt{\sum_{x_j \in \mathcal X_\text{steep}} w_j \,\bigl(\hat f_S(x_j) - f(x_j)\bigr)^2 }, \quad w_j \;=\; \frac{1/x_j}{\sum_k 1/x_k} $$

The weights $w_j \propto |f'(x_j)|$ emphasise locations where the field varies rapidly — exactly the targeted-IMSE tradition of Picheny et al. and Bect et al., specialised to a 1-D field with a known steep region.

The Dataset Quality Score normalises against an arc-length-equidistant benchmark $S^\star$:

$$ \mathrm{DQS}(S) \;=\; \min\!\left\{1,\; \frac{E_\text{steep}^{\,\mathrm{ref}}}{E_\text{steep}(S)}\right\} \;\in\; [0, 1] $$

DQS is scale-free, $\geq 1$ when $S$ matches or beats the benchmark, and clipped at 1 to absorb numerical noise. It follows the regret-vs-best-known convention of Frazier (2018) — the same idea you'd use to benchmark a strategy against a known-best baseline.

GP surrogate with a Matérn-3/2 kernel

The reconstruction operator is a Gaussian process with prior $f \sim \mathcal{GP}(\mu, k)$. Given noisy observations $y = f(S) + \varepsilon$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal N(0, \sigma_n^2 I)$, the posterior is the standard:

$$ \mu_S(x) \;=\; \mu(x) + k_{xS}\bigl(K_{SS} + \sigma_n^2 I\bigr)^{-1}(y - \mu_S) $$

$$ \sigma_S^2(x) \;=\; k(x, x) - k_{xS}\bigl(K_{SS} + \sigma_n^2 I\bigr)^{-1} k_{Sx} $$

The Matérn-3/2 kernel is once mean-square differentiable — appropriate for environmental fields without imposing the over-smoothness of the squared-exponential kernel:

$$ k(x, x') \;=\; \sigma_f^2 \left(1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}\,r}{\ell}\right) \exp\!\left(-\frac{\sqrt{3}\,r}{\ell}\right), \quad r = |x - x'| $$

Modelling salinity in $\ln x$ rather than $x$ keeps the correlation structure near-stationary across the domain, so a single global length-scale $\ell$ serves both the steep near-mouth band and the flat offshore plateau.

Greedy acquisition vs differential evolution

Seven acquisition functions were evaluated on the same protocol — MaxDist, IVR, wIVR, Random, Clustered-Steep, Clustered-Flat, GradMaxVar — at sensor counts $N \in \{3, \ldots, 22\}$ averaged over 30 noise seeds.

For each $N$, differential evolution solves the joint problem:

$$ S^{\mathrm{DE}}(N) \;=\; \arg\min_{|S| = N,\, S \subset \mathcal X} \mathbb E\bigl[E_\text{steep}(S)\bigr] $$

with a Monte Carlo average over noise. DE costs roughly an order of magnitude more than the entire greedy benchmark suite — but it can redistribute previously committed sensors, which greedy methods structurally cannot.

Methods used

surrogate model
Gaussian process, Matérn-3/2 kernel, log-spatial coordinate to preserve stationarity
hyperparameters
L-BFGS-B over the marginal likelihood, 10 Latin-hypercube restarts, warm-started at small $N$
acquisition functions
MaxVar/UCB, EI, PI, IVR, wIVR, GMI, MaxDist, GradMaxVar, plus Clustered-Steep/Flat baselines
global optimiser
Differential evolution (best1bin, pop 50, F ∈ [0.5, 1.0], CR 0.7, 400 generations)
evaluation
Gradient-weighted RMSE on the steep region; Dataset Quality Score normalised against an arc-length-equidistant reference
diagnostics
NLPD and 1-σ posterior-interval coverage as orthogonal calibration scores
protocol
30 seeds for greedy methods, 10 for the optimiser; consistency checks on monotonicity, benchmark reproducibility, residuals

Why I think this matters beyond estuaries

The DQS framework generalises to any field whose scientific value concentrates in an identifiable sub-region. The deeper finding is the one that follows from comparing MaxDist (geometry-only) to EI/PI/UCB (sophisticated, model-based): a method's score is set by the alignment between its internal criterion and the actual scoring rule, not by the sophistication of the criterion. The same point applies to anywhere you have a budget, noise, and a metric that doesn't quite match the loss you're minimising.
